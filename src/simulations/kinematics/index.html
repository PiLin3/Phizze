Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion, and is used to explain the movement of objects without considering what is causing it. It includes concepts such as: distance or displacement, speed or velocity, and acceleration. To start our journey in understanding the motion of objects, first we must be able to distinguish between Distance / Displacement, Speed / Velocity, Scaler and Vector quantities.

Scaler vs Vector quantities

Scalers and vectors are tow concepts, used to describe different quantities. A scaler is a "magnitude" only quantity, it only cares about how much of something there is, for example a tempreture of 25 ℃, an area of 10m2, a distance of 100m, and a speed of 80km/h are all scaler quantities. On the other hand a vector is a "magnitude & direction" quantity , which means it not only cares about how much of something there is but also about its direction, example of vector quantities are a displacement 5m to the east, a force 8N to the left, or a forward acceleration of 2m/s2.

So now after we have been introduced to the concept of scalers and vectors we can explain the difference between distance (a scaler quantity), and displacement (a vector quantity), os if we said that somthing moved a 5 meters distance we don't care if it moved to the right, left, up or down, we just care about the fact that it moved 5 meters, but if something was displaced 5 meters, we must stat if it was displaced to the right, left , up or down. The same difference applies to speed (scaler quantity) vs velocity (vector quantity).

Kinematic Equations

There are Five variables we can solve for using kinematics equation:

  • s: Displacement
  • vo: Initial velocity
  • v: Final velocity
  • a: Constant acceleration
  • t: Time interval

Given that an object is moving with a constant accelereation you can solve for any of the kinematic varibales "displacement, initial/final velocities, time, or acceleration", using the folowing equations:

  • v=vo+at
  • s=vot+(1⁄2)at2
  • a=(2s⁄t2)-(2vo⁄t)

To get an in depth feeling of how they work, you can check the simulation and compare the graphs' output with the equations for each variable.
