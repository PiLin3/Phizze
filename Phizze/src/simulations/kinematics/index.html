Kinematics

Kinematics is the study of motion, and is used to explain the movement of objects without considering what is causing that movement. It includes concepts such as: distance or displacement, speed or velocity, and acceleration. To lay the foundation in understanding the motion of objects, first we must be able to distinguish between Distance / Displacement, Speed / Velocity, or in other words "Scalar and Vector quantities".

Scalar vs Vector quantities

Scalars and vectors are tow concepts, used to describe different quantities. A scalar is a "magnitude" only quantity, it only cares about how much of something there is, for example a tempreture of 25 ℃, an area of 10m2, a distance of 100m, and a speed of 80km/h are all scalar quantities. On the other hand a vector is a "magnitude & direction" quantity , which means it not only cares about how much of something there is but also about its direction, examples of vector quantities are a displacement of 5m to the east, a force of 8N to the left, or a forward acceleration of 2m/s2.

So now after we have been introduced to the concept of scalars and vectors we can explain the difference between distance (a scalar quantity), and displacement (a vector quantity), if we said that somthing moved a distance of 5 meters, we don't care if it moved to the right, left, up or down, we just care about the fact that it moved 5 meters, but if something was displaced 5 meters, we must state if it was displaced to the right, left , up or down. The same difference applies to speed a scalar quantity vs velocity a vector quantity.

Kinematic Equations

There are Five variables we can solve for using the kinematic equations:

  • s: Displacement
  • vo: Initial velocity
  • v: Final velocity
  • a: Constant acceleration
  • t: Time interval

Given that an object is moving with a constant acceleration you can, with some algebric manibulation, solve for any of the kinematic varibales "displacement, initial/final velocities, time, or acceleration", using the folowing equations:

  • v=vo+at
  • s=vot+(1⁄2)at2
  • a=(2s⁄t2)-(2vo⁄t)

To get an in depth feeling of how they work, you can check the simulation and compare the graphs' output with the equations for each variable.
